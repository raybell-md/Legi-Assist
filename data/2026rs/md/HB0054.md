A BILL ENTITLED

1 AN ACT concerning

2 Task Force to Study Restructuring Maryland’s Electric Utility Industry

3 FOR the purpose of establishing the Task Force to Study Restructuring Maryland’s Electric
4 Utility Industry to study and make recommendations on restructuring the State’s
5 electric utility industry; and generally relating to the Task Force to Study
6 Restructuring Maryland’s Electric Utility Industry.

7 SECTION 1. BE IT ENACTED BY THE GENERAL ASSEMBLY OF MARYLAND,
8 That:

9 (a) There is a Task Force to Study Restructuring Maryland’s Electric Utility
10 Industry.

11 (b) The Task Force consists of the following members:

12 (1) one member of the Senate of Maryland, appointed by the President of
13 the Senate;

14 (2) one member of the House of Delegates, appointed by the Speaker of the
15 House;

16 (3) the Secretary of the Environment, or the Secretary’s designee;

17 (4) the Secretary of Transportation, or the Secretary’s designee;

18 (5) the Director of the Maryland Energy Administration, or the Director’s
19 designee;

20 (6) one member of the Public Service Commission, designated by the Chair
21 of the Public Service Commission;

1 (23) one representative of the Consumer Advocates of the PJM States,

3 (24) two representatives of Maryland ratepayers, one appointed by the

10 (c) The Chair of the Public Service Commission shall designate the chair of the
11 Task Force.

12 (d) The Public Service Commission shall provide staff for the Task Force.

13 (e) A member of the Task Force:

14 (1) may not receive compensation as a member of the Task Force; but

15 (2) is entitled to reimbursement for expenses under the Standard State
16 Travel Regulations, as provided in the State budget.

17 (f) The Task Force shall:

18 (1) study options for restructuring Maryland’s electric utility industry,
19 such as publicly owned utilities, cooperatives, and public utility districts;

20 (2) evaluate options for improving or reducing electricity rates for
21 Maryland ratepayers;

22 (3) assess strategies for ensuring that electric companies are more
23 responsive to the needs of ratepayers;

24 (4) examine alternative utility and governance models used in other states,
25 including Nebraska, Washington, and Oregon;

26 (5) compare electricity rates in the states examined under item (4) of this
27 subsection with electricity rates in Maryland;

28 (6) compare infrastructure investments and improvements made by
29 publicly owned electric companies with infrastructure investments and improvements
30 made by private electric companies;
